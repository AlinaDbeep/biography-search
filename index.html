How it works, simply

Before she was Marilyn Monroe, she was Norma Jeane Mortenson — a factory worker assembling parachutes in World War II. Her biography, like everyone else's, is a story.

We gave a computer 1,000 biographical stories to read. It didn't memorize them — it learned to feel how similar they are. Athletes ended up near athletes. Politicians near politicians. Actors found each other across centuries.

The map you're about to explore is the result. Proximity means similarity. When you search for "popular blonde actress", the computer finds the points in the map whose stories feel most like that description — and lights them up.

Embeddings

Each biography is encoded into a 768-dimensional vector using BAAI/bge-base-en-v1.5, a state-of-the-art sentence transformer. The model captures semantic meaning — not just keywords. Two biographies about musicians will have similar vectors even if they share no words.

Dimensionality Reduction

UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) compresses 768 dimensions down to 2 for visualization. It preserves local structure — meaning nearby points in 768D stay nearby in 2D. The clusters you see are real.

Similarity Search

When you type a query, it's embedded with the same model. We then compute cosine similarity between your query vector and all 1,000 biography vectors — returning the closest matches. The full embedding matrix lives in memory for fast lookup.

Infrastructure

Dataset: Wikipedia biographical corpus (~192k people, sampled to 1,000).
Backend: FastAPI + Python.
Embeddings: float16 NumPy array.
Database: PostgreSQL on Google Cloud SQL.
Hosting: Render (coming soon).
